### **1. Fidelity (Accuracy to Original Meaning)**
**Score: 8/10**
**Justification:**
- **Core Meaning Preserved**: The LLM translation captures the essence of the original: freedom/equality at birth and the justification of social distinctions by "common good." Key terms like "生而自由" (born free) and "权利上一律平等" (equal in rights) are accurate.
- **Minor Deviations**:
  - **"Social distinctions can be founded only upon the common good"**:
    - Canonical: "社会上的差别，只能以公共的效用为基础" ("social distinctions can only be based on public utility").
    - LLM: "社会差别只能以公共利益为依归" ("social distinctions can only be guided by public interest").
    - **"公共的效用" (public utility) vs. "公共利益" (public interest)**: While both convey the idea of collective benefit, "效用" (utility) is more precise in legal/philosophical contexts, emphasizing *functional* benefit (a utilitarian nuance), whereas "利益" (interest) is broader and more subjective. The original French *"l'utilité commune"* (common utility) aligns closer with "效用."
  - **Omission of "remain"**: The original states "Men are born *and remain* free," but the LLM omits "remain" (the canonical version also omits it, so this is not a flaw of the LLM).

**Overall Assessment**: The LLM translation is largely faithful but slightly dilutes the utilitarian nuance of "common good" by using "公共利益" instead of "公共的效用."

**Recommendation**: Replace "公共利益" with "公共的效用" to match the original’s legal-philosophical precision.

**Score: 7.5/10**
**Justification:**

    - **"公共的效用" (public utility)** in the canonical version reflects the Enlightenment idea of *functional* social distinctions (e.g., meritocracy), while **"公共利益" (public interest)** is vaguer and could imply subjective interpretations (e.g., political expediency).
  - The omission of "remain" (though also missing in the canonical) subtly understates the *permanence* of equality, which is critical in the original’s intent to reject feudal privileges.
- **Legal Tone**: Both versions use formal language, but the canonical’s "效用" is more aligned with legal/philosophical discourse (e.g., utilitarianism).

**Overall Assessment**: The LLM translation captures the spirit but loses some precision in justifying social distinctions. The shift from "utility" to "interest" could allow for broader (and potentially less rigorous) interpretations.

**Recommendation**: Restore "公共的效用" to preserve the original’s utilitarian nuance and consider adding "并始终" (and always remain) before "自由" to emphasize permanence.
