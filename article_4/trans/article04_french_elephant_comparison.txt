**Score: 9/10**

    The original emphasizes *capacity* ("pouvoir faire"), while the LLM adds redundancy by repeating "liberté" (freedom). This slightly alters the focus from *ability* to *freedom as a concept*.

    "Chacun" is a neutral modernization, but "chaque homme" in the original carries a gendered (though historically contextual) weight, emphasizing individual rights.
  - **"aux autres membres" → "à tous les membres"**:
    The original specifies "other members" (excluding the self), while the LLM’s "tous" (all) could imply inclusivity of the actor, which is less precise.

**Overall Assessment:**
The LLM translation is highly faithful but loses some nuance in legal precision and philosophical tone. The deviations are minor but slightly weaken the original’s emphasis on *limits* and *individual capacity*.

### **3. Intent Preservation (Legal-Philosophical Nuance)**
**Score: 8/10**
**Justification:**
- The original’s intent is to define liberty as *freedom from harm to others*, with *law as the sole arbiter of limits*. The LLM preserves this but with subtle shifts:
  - **"la liberté consiste à pouvoir faire" → "consiste en la liberté de faire"**:
    The original frames liberty as *an inherent capacity* ("pouvoir"), while the LLM frames it as *a freedom granted by definition*. This subtly changes the philosophical emphasis from *natural rights* to *defined rights*.
  - **"ne nuit pas à autrui" → "n’porte pas préjudice à autrui"**:
    "Nuire" is a moral/ethical term, while "préjudice" is a legal term. The original’s choice aligns with Enlightenment philosophy (e.g., Rousseau), while the LLM’s leans toward modern legalese.
  - **"aux autres membres" → "à tous les membres"**:
    The original’s "others" reinforces the idea of *reciprocal limits*, while "tous" could imply a collective obligation, diluting the individualist focus.

**Overall Assessment:**
The LLM captures the legal intent but weakens the philosophical depth. The original’s language is more aligned with natural law theory (rights as inherent, limits as reciprocal), while the LLM’s is more positivist (rights as defined by law).
