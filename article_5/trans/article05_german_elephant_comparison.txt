### **1. Fidelity (Accuracy to Original Meaning)**
**Score: 8/10**
**Justification:**
- **Core meaning preserved**: The LLM translation captures the essence of the original, particularly the three key principles:
  1. Laws may only prohibit actions harmful to society.
  2. Nothing may be prohibited unless forbidden by law.
  3. No one may be forced to act unless required by law.
- **Deviations**:
  - *"Nur das Gesetz hat das Recht"* (canonical) → *"Das Gesetz kann nur"* (LLM):
    The original emphasizes the **exclusive authority** of the law ("only the law has the right"), while the LLM version softens this to a **capacity** ("the law can only"). This weakens the legal-philosophical exclusivity of legislative power.
  - *"zu verbieten"* (infinitive, canonical) → *"verbieten"* (finite verb, LLM):
    The infinitive construction in the original is more abstract and authoritative, while the LLM’s finite verb ("kann verbieten") sounds more like a description of capability than a normative statement.
  - *"was nicht durch das Gesetz verboten ist"* (canonical) → *"was nicht vom Gesetz verboten ist"* (LLM):
    Minor stylistic difference; both are correct, but the canonical version is slightly more formal.
  - *"niemand darf gezwungen werden, etwas zu tun, was das Gesetz nicht vorschreibt"* (canonical) → *"niemand kann gezwungen werden, etwas zu tun, was nicht durch das Gesetz vorgesehen ist"* (LLM):
    The LLM replaces *"vorschreibt"* (prescribes) with *"vorgesehen ist"* (provided for). While similar, *"vorschreiben"* is stronger and more direct in legal contexts, whereas *"vorgesehen"* can imply mere inclusion rather than a binding obligation.

**Overall Assessment**:
The LLM translation is **largely accurate** but loses some of the original’s **normative force** and **legal precision**. It reads more like a paraphrase than a strict translation.

**Recommendations**:
- Restore *"Nur das Gesetz hat das Recht"* to preserve the exclusivity of legislative authority.
- Use *"vorschreibt"* instead of *"vorgesehen ist"* for stronger legal clarity.
- Consider keeping the infinitive constructions (*"zu verbieten"*) for consistency with legal drafting conventions.

### **3. Intent Preservation (Legal-Philosophical Nuances)**
**Score: 7/10**
**Justification**:
The original text reflects **three foundational principles of the rule of law**:
1. **Legality of prohibitions**: Only the law can prohibit actions, and only if they harm society.
2. **Nullum crimen sine lege**: Nothing is forbidden unless the law explicitly says so.
3. **Nulla poena sine lege**: No one can be forced to act unless the law requires it.

**Where the LLM succeeds**:
- It preserves the **substantive intent** of all three principles.
- The structure is logically equivalent to the original.

**Where the LLM falls short**:
- **Exclusivity of law**: The canonical *"Nur das Gesetz hat das Recht"* is a **stronger assertion of legislative supremacy** than *"Das Gesetz kann nur"*, which sounds more like a limitation on the law’s power (rather than an affirmation of its exclusive authority).
- **Normative force**: The original is **prescriptive** (what *must* be), while the LLM version is **descriptive** (what *can* be). This shifts the tone from a **binding legal principle** to a **general statement about the law’s function**.
- *"vorgesehen ist"* vs. *"vorschreibt"*: The latter is **mandatory** ("the law prescribes"), while the former is **permissive** ("the law provides for"). This weakens the principle that the law is the sole source of obligations.

**Overall Assessment**:
The LLM translation **captures the spirit** of the original but **dilutes its legal-philosophical rigor**. It would be acceptable for general use but less suitable for constitutional or high-stakes legal documents.

**Recommendations**:
- Restore the **exclusive authority of the law** (*"Nur das Gesetz hat das Recht"*).
- Use **stronger normative language** (*"vorschreibt"* instead of *"vorgesehen ist"*).
- Avoid introducing new terms (*"verhindert"*) that could create ambiguity.
