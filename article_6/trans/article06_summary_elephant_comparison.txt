## **Recommendations for Improving Translation Quality**

### **1. Improve Handling of Abstract Legal-Philosophical Terms**
- **Problem:** "General will" (Rousseau) is **mistranslated in Chinese, Arabic, Japanese**.
- **Solution:**
  - **Fine-tune training data** to include **more legal-philosophical texts** in these languages.
  - **Add a glossary of key terms** (e.g., "general will" = "公意" in Chinese, "الإرادة العامة" in Arabic, "一般意志の表現" in Japanese).
  - **Implement post-editing rules** to flag and correct such errors.

### **2. Enhance Consistency in Formal Legal Phrasing**
- **Problem:** Some translations **modernize too much** (e.g., "punir" → "sanctionner" in French).
- **Solution:**
  - **Train on historical legal texts** to preserve **formal legal tone**.
  - **Use a style guide** to enforce **canonical phrasing** where applicable.

### **3. Strengthen Non-European Language Support**
- **Problem:** **Chinese, Arabic, Japanese** perform **significantly worse** than European languages.
- **Solution:**
  - **Increase training data** for **legal and philosophical texts** in these languages.
  - **Collaborate with native legal experts** to refine translations.
  - **Implement language-specific fine-tuning** to improve abstract term handling.

### **4. Implement Human-in-the-Loop Post-Editing**
- **Problem:** Even minor errors (e.g., "formation" → "elaboration") can **reduce precision**.
- **Solution:**
  - **Use Elephant for first drafts**, then **apply human post-editing** for critical documents.
  - **Develop an AI-assisted review system** to flag potential deviations.

### **5. Expand Domain-Specific Training**
- **Problem:** Legal translations require **high precision** in terminology.
- **Solution:**
  - **Train on more legal corpora** (e.g., constitutions, UN documents, historical legal texts).
  - **Fine-tune for legal subdomains** (e.g., constitutional law, human rights law).
